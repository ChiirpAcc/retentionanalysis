You have a specific deal in front of you and want to see what changing one variable does. Lock the variables that are non-negotiable, then optimize the rest.
When to use the explorer

Shared model
Both tools use the same compact churn model (pseudo R² = 0.0218, N = 14,208) and the 0.3854 calibration factor on analytical LTV. Use the rankings and relative differences, not absolute predictions.

Key findings
The five things to know before approving a deal in this MRR band.
›
Headline finding · onboarding sweet spot

Onboarding helps churn — but only up to about 100% of MRR

The model says onboarding has a U-shaped effect on churn. Going from no onboarding to 100% of MRR cuts predicted 12-month churn dramatically. Going above 100% slowly raises predicted churn again, because the squared onboarding term in the regression gets larger.

At MRR $1,500, 0 free months, the model predicts:

  • 0% onboarding → ~71% 12-mo churn
  • 50% onboarding → ~44%
  • 100% onboarding → ~32% (lowest)
  • 125% onboarding → ~34%
  • 150% onboarding → ~37%
  • 200% onboarding → ~43%

Operational read: 100% of MRR is the churn-optimal onboarding charge. You can charge 125–150% and trade ~2–5 percentage points of churn for meaningfully more upfront cash and faster payback. Going past 150% should require a strategic reason — the model is no longer rewarding you for the extra collection.

A · MRR

Higher MRR strengthens economics, but does not lower churn much in this band

Across $1,000 → $2,250 MRR, predicted 12-mo churn at 100% onboarding moves only from ~33% to ~36%. MRR is the biggest economic lever (LTV scales with it directly) but not a meaningful churn lever inside this band.

Practical: don't discount MRR to "save" churn. The churn change is small and the LTV cost is large.

B · Onboarding cash timing

Onboarding shortens CAC payback more than any other lever

Onboarding cash hits on day 1 and counts toward CAC payback immediately. A $1,500 deal with 100% onboarding recovers $1,500 of CAC up front before any subscription revenue. The same deal at 0% onboarding has to earn that first $1,500 from MRR while still bleeding higher early-month churn.

C · Waived months

Waived months are doubly costly

They delay MRR and the model attaches a churn penalty to them. The 2-month waiver in particular has the strongest churn signal in the regression (free_2mo_exact, +0.32 log-odds).

  • 0 free months: clean baseline
  • 1 free month: small but tolerable
  • 2 free months: clearest churn penalty in the data — treat as exception-only
  • 3+ free months: shouldn't be standard

E · CAC sensitivity

CAC threshold changes what's viable, not the curves

The churn and LTV curves don't depend on CAC. CAC just sets the bar. At higher CAC, the same deal structure can drop from "healthy" to "underwater" without anything else changing — which is why waived-month tolerance should tighten when CAC rises.

Selected CAC: $5,000. To clear 3× LTV:CAC at this CAC, a deal needs $15,000 calibrated LTV.

Decision rules we should adopt
Operational guardrails reps and approvers can apply without re-running the model.
›
Rule 1 — Onboarding floor of 100% of MRR Default
100% is the model's churn-optimal point. Quoting below 100% should be the exception, not the standard, and should require a clear reason (multi-year commitment, strategic logo, etc.).
Rule 2 — Onboarding ceiling of 150% unless justified Soft cap
Between 100% and 150% the churn cost is small (~2–5 pp) and the cash benefit is real. Above 150%, the model says you're paying for it in churn without a payback gain that clearly justifies it.
Rule 3 — Treat 2+ waived months as exception-only Approval
The regression's strongest free-month signal is at exactly 2 waived months. If a deal needs them, it should be offset by 125–150% onboarding and stay above the LTV:CAC and payback targets.
Rule 4 — Use LTV:CAC and payback together
A deal with strong long-term LTV:CAC can still pay back too slowly to be safe. Require both checks before approving anything non-standard.

5
Model confidence & caveats
Read this before treating any single prediction as truth.
›

This is a directional decision-support model, not an account-level forecast. Decisions should rest on relative comparisons and guardrails, not point predictions.

Stable, modest fitThe compact churn regression is stable (no NaN standard errors, N = 14,208) but pseudo-R² is only ~0.022. It captures direction well; magnitudes should be treated as approximate.
Waiver-post-sign held at 0The model has a separate term for "onboarding contracted but waived after signing." The dashboard does not separately observe this, so we hold that variable at 0. If you start tracking post-sign waivers, churn predictions for those deals will worsen.
LTV is calibrated, not rawAnalytical LTV from churn survival is multiplied by the calibration factor 0.3854, the median actual/analytical ratio observed historically. This corrects for analytical LTV's tendency to overstate.
Use rankings, not absolute LTVTwo scenarios that differ by $500 of predicted LTV are essentially indistinguishable in this model. Two scenarios that differ by $5,000 are meaningfully different.
