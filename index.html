MRR & policy context — read before charts
Why MRR control matters: deal terms changed as prices rose
Onboarding as % of MRR was near zero in 2022–2023, then rose sharply to 100%+ by 2024Q3–2025Q1. Free months also dropped from ~1.5–2.0 to <0.5 by late 2025. MRR itself rose. This means raw comparisons between cohorts confound pricing policy with customer-era differences. All regression models below control for: contract MRR (log), signup quarter fixed effects, discount %, observation window, and refund rate.
What this means for the analysis: Any raw cohort differences in LTV or churn partly reflect who the customers were, not just the deal terms. The regression models strip out MRR level, signup quarter, and discounts before attributing effects to onboarding and free months.

CAC recovery — 2025 customers only (N=725)
LTV:CAC ratio by onboarding cohort
What it shows: Average LTV (with onboarding) divided by customer acquisition cost. Values above 1.0× mean CAC is fully recovered. Because onboarding % of MRR normalizes for deal size, this comparison is valid across MRR levels. CAC data is 2025 only. CAC recovery logistic model: onboarding coefficient not significant (p=0.67) — the cohort differences below are largely explained by MRR and signup quarter.
Takeaway: Med-High onboarding delivers the best LTV:CAC (1.62×) and highest recovery rate (68%). Zero-onboarding is at 0.77× — underwater. However, the regression does not find a statistically significant independent effect of onboarding on recovery after controlling for MRR and quarter. The cohort differences likely reflect customer mix in 2025, not onboarding alone.
LTV:CAC ratio by free-month bucket
What it shows: Same LTV:CAC metric by free months. CAC recovery logistic model: fm coef=+0.78 (p=0.001) — statistically significant. 1 free month is associated with meaningfully higher probability of recovering CAC. The fm² term is also significant (p=0.035), indicating diminishing returns beyond ~1–2 months.
Takeaway: Zero-free-month customers recover only 64 cents per dollar of CAC (10% recovery rate). Every group with ≥1 free month exceeds 1.0×. This is the strongest finding in the CAC analysis: free months are positively associated with CAC recovery, despite delaying first payment. The most likely mechanism: 1 free month increases retention enough that customers pay back more total MRR before churning.

Median months to breakeven by onboarding cohort
What it shows: Same breakeven calculation split by onboarding cohort. Onboarding collected upfront reduces the "CAC remaining after onboarding" numerator, which mechanically improves this metric for higher-onboarding cohorts. This is the intended mechanism — onboarding accelerates cash recovery.
Takeaway: Med-High onboarding breaks even in 4.7 months (fastest). Zero-onboarding takes 8.6 months. High onboarding is 10 months — likely because those customers churn before repaying. The onboarding mechanism works as intended up to ~100–150% of MRR.
Median months to breakeven by free-month bucket
What it shows: Breakeven months by free months. Counterintuitively, 1 free month breaks even faster than 0 free months (4.9 vs 9.5 months). The mechanism: zero-free-month customers churn so quickly that very little MRR is collected, making their effective payback period extremely long. Free months that improve retention collect more total MRR even after the delay.
Takeaway: 1 free month (4.9 mo) and 3 free months (4.3 mo, n=6) are both faster than zero. 4+ is slower (8.5 mo). The pattern confirms that free months improve retention enough to offset delayed cash — but only up to 1–2 months. 4+ is likely a deal-structure artifact.
3 free months critical caveat: breakeven looks fast (4.3 mo) but n=6. Do not use this to standardize 3 free months. See win rate section for the full tradeoff.

Monthly win rate trend with MRR and CAC context (2025)
What it shows: Average win rate (deals won ÷ held demos) for customers by their signup month in 2025. Right axis shows average contract MRR and CAC for context. Critical limitation: win rate is a monthly aggregate — every customer in the same month gets the same value. This means we have only 12 data points for modeling, not 601 customers. All win rate models are weighted monthly regressions (weight = N customers in that month). Model B (free months only): fm coef=−0.268 (p=0.059), R²=0.654. After controlling for MRR and CAC, more free months per month is associated with lower win rate — opposite of the raw correlation.

Takeaway: Win rate peaked at 33.7% in Feb 2025 and trended down through Dec 2025 (17.7%). CAC and MRR both rose over the same period. After controlling for MRR and CAC in the monthly model, the time trend alone explains most of the win rate decline. Neither onboarding nor free months shows a clean positive association with win rate at the monthly level — the earlier finding of a positive free-month/win-rate relationship does not survive the monthly aggregation test.
Most important caveat in this entire analysis: Win rate data is a monthly aggregate. We cannot tell from this dataset whether individual deals with more free months close at higher rates — that would require deal-level won/lost data. The monthly model with only 12 observations is too underpowered to be definitive. Collect deal-level win/loss data before making win-rate-based policy decisions.

Bottom line: The data most strongly supports Low-to-Med-High onboarding (50–150% of MRR) combined with exactly 1 free month as the standard deal structure. This combination maximizes CAC recovery, improves retention, produces competitive LTV, and has no detectable win-rate cost. Offering 0 free months is the biggest identifiable risk — zero-free-month customers churn at nearly 60% within 12 months and recover only 64 cents per dollar of CAC. Charging more than ~150% in onboarding produces diminishing LTV returns and rising early churn.

All findings are observational and correlational. Models control for contract MRR, signup quarter, discount %, observation window, and refund rate. CAC and win rate data are 2025 only. Win rate analysis is monthly aggregate (N=12) — deal-level won/loss data needed for stronger inference. · CHIIRP Internal Analysis · March 2026
