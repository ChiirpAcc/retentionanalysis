Why MRR control is non-negotiable
Onboarding % of MRR was effectively zero before mid-2023, then rose to 100%+ by late 2024. Free months simultaneously dropped from ~1.5–2.0 to under 0.5. Contract MRR itself doubled. Any raw comparison between deal structures without controlling for this will attribute period effects to deal terms. All analysis in this dashboard uses: onboarding as % of MRR (not absolute dollars), log(MRR) as a covariate, and signup-quarter fixed effects.
What the chart shows: Three simultaneous policy shifts happened from 2023→2025: MRR rose, onboarding was introduced, and free months decreased. These are correlated in time with churn improvements — but the regression models below attempt to separate each variable's independent contribution.
Average MRR by deal structure (MRR-tier confound)
Each bar shows the median MRR of customers in that deal structure. The structures are not comparable on raw LTV — they serve systematically different customer sizes. This is why the heatmap's "127.8× LTV/MRR" for Zero OB + 1FM is misleading: that cell contains 44% pre-2023 customers paying $75–$300/month who have accumulated many years of payments.
Key insight: Med-High OB customers have median MRR $650. Zero OB customers have median MRR $300. Comparing their outcomes directly conflates deal-term effects with customer-size effects.
12-month churn by MRR tier (within same structure)
Within the same MRR tier, how does deal structure affect churn? This controls for the customer-size confound. Among Mid MRR ($300–600) customers: Low OB + 1FM churns at 30.3% vs Zero OB + 1FM at 37.2% — a real 7pp difference at the same price level.
Key insight: The churn advantage of "Med-High OB + 1FM" over "Zero OB + 1FM" at the same MRR tier (35.2% vs 41.9% in the >$600 group) is ~6.7pp — real but smaller than the raw heatmap suggests. Within Mid MRR, Low OB + 1FM wins.

Policy variable trends — 6-month rolling averages

Each line is a 6-month rolling average to smooth month-to-month noise. Directional shifts of ≥3 months are meaningful.

⚠ Policy variables are correlated in time. Rising MRR, rising onboarding %, and falling free months all happened together. Use the rolling averages to see direction, not to infer causality.
Avg contract MRR over time (6-month rolling)
Monthly average contracted MRR for new signups. This is the primary control variable — all other metrics need to be read alongside it. The sharp rise in 2026 reflects a small number of large enterprise deals and may not represent the typical sales motion.
Onboarding % of MRR — 6-month rolling avg
Average onboarding fee as % of first-month MRR. This is the MRR-normalized measure — it strips out price inflation so a $600 deal with 100% onboarding and a $300 deal with 100% onboarding are treated equally. Direction: peaked mid-2025, declining into late 2025–2026.
Avg free months — 6-month rolling avg
Average number of free months given at contract start. Direction: broadly declining from ~1.5 in 2022–2023 toward ~0.2 by early 2026. The fall in free months happened as onboarding rose — these are partially substituting for each other as sales tools.
% of deals with onboarding > 0
Share of new signups where any onboarding fee was charged. Near zero before 2023, accelerated to 80–90% in early 2025, now declining. Late 2025 shows a drop in onboarding penetration coinciding with the rise in avg MRR (suggesting larger deals may be getting different terms).
CAC by signup month (2025 +)
Average customer acquisition cost by signup month, available only for 2025 onward. CAC roughly doubled from H1 to H2 2025 ($4k → $8k+). This is the structural backdrop: the deal structure that recovers CAC fastest matters more as CAC rises. CAC data ends at current observation window.

Regression-adjusted marginal effects

After controlling for MRR (log scale), signup quarter, and refund rate — how does each additional unit of onboarding or free months shift outcomes?

Effect of onboarding % on LTV
LTV model · R²=0.18 · N=2,863 · ob p=0.022, ob² p=0.014
Each point is the marginal effect at that level of onboarding (additional log-LTV per 10pp of onboarding). Positive = more LTV. The quadratic term (ob²) captures diminishing returns — the line crosses zero at ~98% of MRR, the inflection point beyond which adding more onboarding starts to hurt total LTV.
Turning point: ~98% of MRR. At 50% onboarding, each additional 10pp adds ~+4.3% to LTV. At 100%, you're at the inflection — returns are near zero. At 150%, you're destroying LTV. Statistically significant (p=0.022/0.014).
Effect of free months on LTV
LTV model · fm p=0.076, fm² p=0.012
Effect of each additional free month on log-LTV, controlling for MRR and onboarding. Positive and declining — each free month adds LTV (through retention) but at a decreasing rate. The effect stays positive across the observed range (0–7 months), unlike onboarding which turns negative.
Each additional free month adds ~+15% to LTV at month 0, declining to ~+9% by month 7. This is a direct LTV effect from the model — it reflects improved retention more than it reflects delayed cash. fm² is significant (p=0.012), confirming the concave shape.
Effect of onboarding % on 12-month churn probability
Churn model (LPM) · R²=0.01 · N=1,319 (2023+ cohorts) · not statistically significant
Each point is the marginal change in 12-month churn probability per unit of onboarding. Negative = less churn. The small R² (0.01) and lack of statistical significance (p=0.33) mean this model is not reliable for precise predictions. The direction is right — more onboarding correlates with less churn — but the effect is too noisy to quantify confidently after controlling for MRR.
Low confidence. The churn model has R²=0.01. Most of the churn variation is explained by factors not in this model — sales process, product fit, customer success, use-case match. Deal terms explain a small portion. Use the observed cohort data (Lifetime Value tab) as the more reliable signal.
Effect of free months on 12-month churn probability
Churn model (LPM) · fm not significant (p=0.36)
Marginal change in churn probability per additional free month, after controlling for MRR and onboarding. The fm² coefficient is near zero and not significant — the churn model does not find a reliable nonlinear free-month effect after MRR control. The observed cohort differences in churn by free-month bucket are more reliable than this regression.
Low confidence. After controlling for MRR, the regression finds no statistically significant free-month effect on churn. The large observed difference (zero vs 1 free month: 59.9% vs 41.5% churn) is likely driven by customer-era differences, not free months per se.
