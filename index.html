LTV vs. onboarding — what the data actually shows
Normalised LTV multiple across onboarding fee levels

Bars = historical geometric mean LTV per bin (what the data directly shows). Green line = model prediction at each bin's actual average composition. The 0% onboarding group's apparent high LTV is a composition artifact — they have 16-month average billing spans vs 10.5 months for others. The genuine onboarding signal is the step up from ~8× at 50% to ~11–13× at 100–150%. Raw onboarding explains <1% of LTV variance alone; after controlling for billing span, MRR, and leakage, R² = 57%.

What the data shows The raw effect of onboarding on LTV is nearly flat between 0% and 50% (both ~8×). It genuinely rises to 11–13× at 100–150%, then falls at 200%+. This step-up is real — not a model artifact. The model's advantage is explaining why the 0% group doesn't look worse: those accounts are older, lower-MRR, and longer-tenured. Once you hold those constant, the onboarding slope is steep.

What this is not
Limits worth stating
  • Observational, not causal. Selection between deal types is not random; the coefficients are best controlled estimates given the history.
  • Win rate is fixed by design. Held at 11.5% baseline so optimization can't game the win-rate equation. Observed monthly win rates ranged 17.6% – 33.7% in 2025; the chosen baseline is conservative.
  • MRR's churn coefficient is not statistically significant (p=0.22) but is included for product reasons — keeping MRR in churn enables proper optimization across all three levers.
  • The 1-free-month bin dominates the data. 1,613 of 2,404 churn-eligible accounts. 0, 2, and 3 free-month bins are smaller.
  • CAC is a threshold, not a behavioural input. Higher CAC moves the breakeven line upward; it does not change predicted churn or LTV.
