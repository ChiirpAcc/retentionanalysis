5, 6 & 7 — CAC recovery (2025 customers only, n=725)
Median months to CAC breakeven — by onboarding cohort
How long the average customer takes to pay back their acquisition cost, accounting for onboarding revenue collected upfront. Formula: (CAC − onboarding collected) ÷ avg monthly MRR collected. Controlling for onboarding % of MRR means MRR-level differences are absorbed into the denominator. Shorter = better.
Key takeaway: Med-High onboarding (100–154% of MRR) breaks even in a median of 4.7 months — the fastest of any group. Zero-onboarding takes 8.6 months. High onboarding takes 10 months, possibly because those customers churn early before repaying. Using onboarding % of MRR as the measure controls for the fact that higher-MRR customers now pay higher absolute onboarding fees.
Median months to CAC breakeven — by free-month bucket
Same breakeven metric by free months. Note: free months delay the first MRR payment, which mechanically extends breakeven. The question is whether the improved retention offsets the delay. Compare breakeven months here against the LTV and survival curves above.
Key takeaway: 1 free month breaks even in 4.9 months — faster than zero free months (9.5 months). This seems counterintuitive but reflects that zero-free-month customers churn faster and collect less total MRR. 3 free months = 4.3 months (fastest), though n=6. 4+ months = 8.5 months, reflecting longer delay before payments begin.
⚠ 3 free months is the most important tradeoff: breakeven looks fast here (n=6), but if win rate doesn't increase materially, offering 3+ free months delays cash and creates CAC risk. See win rate charts below before drawing conclusions.
CAC breakeven trend by signup quarter (MRR-aware)
Median months to breakeven for customers signing up each quarter in 2025–2026. This is the cleanest MRR-controlled view: because we're using breakeven months (a ratio of CAC to MRR collected), MRR growth over time is partially absorbed. The trend here captures real changes in CAC efficiency, not just pricing inflation. Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 customers are immature — their longer breakeven times partly reflect incomplete payment histories, not just worse economics. Average MRR is shown as a secondary line for context.

Key takeaway: Q1 2025 had the fastest breakeven (4.2 months) and the most mature data. Breakeven has lengthened through the year. Part of this is a maturity artifact — Q4 2025 customers haven't had enough time to pay back CAC. Q4 also shows rising CAC ($8k avg). 8, 9 & 10 — Win rate (2025 only, n=601, MRR-controlled)
Win rate by month (2025)
Monthly average win rate (deals won ÷ held demos) for customers in our dataset, by the month they signed up. Win rate data is only available for 2025. Average contract MRR for that month is shown as a secondary line to illustrate that rising MRR (harder sell) may explain some win rate pressure — not onboarding or free-month policy alone. The regression model (R²=0.16) confirms that higher MRR is associated with lower win rate (p<0.001), while higher onboarding % is associated with slightly higher win rate (p<0.001) and more free months also correlates with higher win rate (p=0.001).
Win rate (%)
Avg MRR (right axis)
Key takeaway: Win rate peaked in Feb 2025 (33.7%) and has trended down through Dec 2025 (17.7%). MRR has risen over the same period — higher-priced deals are harder to close. The regression controls for this: after adjusting for MRR, more onboarding is associated with higher win rate and more free months are also associated with higher win rate. This is the most important caveat: onboarding and free months may improve close rates enough to justify their cost.
⚠ Critical limitation: Win rate data is a monthly aggregate — every customer in the same month gets the same win rate value. This means these are period-level correlations, not customer-level causation. More data (deal-level win/loss) is needed to make this analysis definitive.
Win rate by onboarding cohort (MRR-adjusted, 2025)
Average win rate for 2025 customers in each onboarding cohort, adjusted for contract MRR using OLS residuals. Raw win rates are shown as lighter bars for comparison. The adjustment removes the effect of higher-MRR customers being in certain cohorts — so this shows whether onboarding % itself is associated with close rate, independent of deal size.

Key takeaway: Low onboarding cohort (<57% of MRR) has the highest adjusted win rate (27.6%). This may reflect that lower-resistance prospects convert more readily at lower price points. There is no clear monotonic relationship — Med-Low has the lowest rate. More data is needed before setting onboarding-as-price policy based on win rate alone.
Win rate by free-month bucket (MRR-adjusted, 2025)
Same MRR-adjusted win rate by free months. The regression coefficient on free months is positive and significant (coef=+0.0067, p=0.001) — each additional free month is associated with ~0.7pp higher win rate, after controlling for MRR. This is the key tradeoff chart for the exec team.
MRR-adjusted win rate
Raw win rate
Key takeaway: 3 free months has the highest adjusted win rate (26.2%), but n=6 — too small to be definitive. 1 and 2 free months are roughly equivalent on win rate (23.3% / 24.3%). The tradeoff: 3+ free months may win slightly more deals, but significantly delays CAC recovery (see Section 7). 1 free month appears to be the best balance across all three outcomes.
11 — Policy matrix: onboarding cohort × free-month bucket
Deal structure outcomes heatmap
Each cell shows outcomes for customers with that combination of onboarding level and free months. Top number = avg LTV ÷ MRR ratio (higher = more revenue per dollar of MRR). Middle = 12-month churn rate. Bottom = avg LTV:CAC ratio (where available; requires 2025 CAC data). Color encodes LTV:CAC — green cells are profitable, red are underwater. Cells with fewer than 5 customers are shown as empty. Use this as a quick-scan guide for which deal structures to standardize vs. avoid.

Cell format: LTV/MRR ratio · 12-mo churn % · LTV:CAC (if available). Color = LTV:CAC performance.

Key takeaway: The strongest cells are Low onboarding + 1 free month (LTV:CAC 1.94x, 43.6% churn) and Low + 2 free months (LTV:CAC 2.60x, 10% churn — but small n). Med-High + 0 free months also stands out (LTV:CAC 1.65x, 39.8% churn). The worst outcomes are High onboarding + 0 free months (100% churn, 0.37x LTV:CAC) and Med-Low + 0 free months (81.3% churn). Standard recommendation: Low-to-Med-High onboarding with 1 free month.
12 — Predicted marginal effects on the Big 3
Marginal effect of onboarding % on each outcome
From the regression models, how each additional 10 percentage points of onboarding (as % of MRR) changes each of the three outcomes, holding MRR and free months constant. The LTV effect peaks around 80% and turns negative. The churn effect was not statistically significant. The win rate effect is positive (+0.002 per 10pp), meaning higher onboarding doesn't appear to hurt close rates in 2025.

Optimal onboarding: 80–100% of MRR. This maximizes LTV, doesn't harm win rate (positive coefficient), and sits in the zone where CAC recovery is fastest. Charging above ~150% of MRR has diminishing or negative LTV returns.
Marginal effect of free months on each outcome
How each additional free month shifts each outcome. The LTV effect is mildly negative (each free month slightly reduces LTV in isolation — the direct revenue delay effect). The churn effect peaks in benefit around 1 month (statistically significant squared term, p=0.046). The win rate effect is positive and significant (+0.67pp per free month after MRR control).

Optimal free months: 1. Win rate benefit is real but modest per additional month. Churn benefit peaks near 1 month. LTV cost accumulates with each additional free month. The complete picture — win rate + churn + LTV + CAC — all point to 1 free month as the policy sweet spot. If win rate is the primary constraint, test offering 2 months on a subset before standardizing.
⚠ 3 free months warning: win rate benefit (+2pp) vs. CAC delay (+4 months to breakeven) is a poor tradeoff at current CAC levels ($5.9k avg). Only justified for high-MRR deals (>$1k/mo) where the math tilts favorably.
