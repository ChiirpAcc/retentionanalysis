When to use the calculator
You have a specific deal in front of you and want to see what changing one variable does. Lock the variables that are non-negotiable, then optimize the rest.
When to use the explorer
You want to compare deal structures directionally across many configurations. Best for setting pricing policy or finding the best structure for a goal metric.
When to use the forecast
You want to model total pipeline economics — how many deals close, how many survive year 1, and what net revenue looks like across the whole pipeline.
Important: All three tools project from the same two models: a 42-feature L2 logistic churn model (AUC 0.78, N=2,944) and an 8-feature ElasticNet win-rate model (LOO R²=0.45, N=14 months). Treat outputs as directional. Use rankings and relative differences, not absolute predictions.

Key findings
The five things to know before approving a deal in this MRR band.
›
Headline finding · onboarding raises churn and kills win rate

Higher onboarding increases churn monotonically — and collapses win rate above ~25%

The L2 logistic churn model has both ob_pct (coef +0.116) and ob_pct_sq (coef +0.624) as positive — meaning more onboarding always raises predicted churn. There is no U-shape minimum at 100%. The churn minimum is near 25% OB. At the same time, the win-rate model has a large negative coefficient on avg_ob_pct (−0.204), meaning higher onboarding also reduces the probability of winning the deal — hitting the 5% model floor around OB=75%.

At MRR $1,500, 0 free months, the churn model predicts:

  • 0% onboarding → ~34% 12-mo churn
  • 25% onboarding → ~33% (model minimum)
  • 50% onboarding → ~34%
  • 100% onboarding → ~43%
  • 150% onboarding → ~62%
  • 200% onboarding → ~84%

Operational read: Onboarding is a cash and payback lever, not a churn lever. Lower onboarding improves both churn and win rate — the trade-off is upfront cash and payback speed. Use the Forecast tab to find the OB level that maximizes expected pipeline LTV for your actual CAC and pipeline size.

A · MRR

Higher MRR lowers churn and strengthens all economics

The model's high_mrr (coef −0.293) and log_mrr (coef −0.153) both reduce churn for larger accounts. LTV scales directly with MRR, so higher MRR improves every metric simultaneously.

Practical: don't discount MRR to close — churn improves with higher MRR in this model, so discounting is doubly costly.

B · Onboarding cash timing

Onboarding accelerates payback, but at a churn and win-rate cost

Onboarding cash hits on day 1 and counts toward CAC payback immediately. A $1,500 deal at 100% OB recovers $578 of calibrated CAC upfront. But the same deal at 100% OB has ~43% predicted churn and a win-rate model output at the 5% floor — meaning the expected number of won deals is very low. Model the full pipeline, not just one won deal.

C · Waived months

Waived months consistently raise churn — the jump at 2 months is sharp

At MRR $1,500 with 0% OB, the churn model predicts:

  • 0 free months: ~34%
  • 1 free month: ~39%
  • 2 free months: ~57% — large jump
  • 3+ free months: ~75%

The 1→2 free month step is the biggest single increase. Treat 2+ waived months as exception-only.

E · CAC sensitivity

CAC threshold changes what's viable, not the curves

The churn and LTV curves don't depend on CAC. CAC just sets the bar. When CAC is high, a low-OB structure (with its higher win rate) may generate more total won deals whose LTV collectively exceeds CAC spend — even though per-deal payback is slower.

Selected CAC: $5,000. To clear 3× LTV:CAC at this CAC, a deal needs $15,000 calibrated LTV.

Decision rules we should adopt
Operational guardrails reps and approvers can apply without re-running the model.
›
Rule 1 — Onboarding of 0–50% minimizes churn Churn lens
The new model's churn minimum is near 25% OB — not 100%. If reducing churn is the primary goal, quoting 0–50% OB is better than quoting 100%+. Higher OB raises churn monotonically in this model.
Rule 2 — Onboarding above ~75% kills win rate Win rate lens
The win-rate model hits its 5% floor around OB=75%. Deals quoted above that level are predicted to rarely close. If you need to charge high OB, expect very low win rate and plan pipeline size accordingly.
Rule 3 — Treat 2+ waived months as exception-only Approval
The 1→2 free month step is the largest single churn jump in the model (~39% → ~57% at MRR $1,500). If a deal needs 2 free months, it should be offset by other negotiated terms and stay above the LTV:CAC and payback targets.
Rule 4 — Use LTV:CAC and win rate together
A deal structure with excellent per-deal LTV:CAC can still be a bad pipeline strategy if win rate is at 5%. Always evaluate expected pipeline LTV (LTV × win rate × pipeline size) alongside per-deal economics.
Rule 5 — Use the Forecast tab for OB decisions Tool
The optimal OB level depends on your actual CAC, pipeline size, and whether you're optimizing per-deal payback or total pipeline value. The Forecast tab models all of this explicitly with both scoring models.

Policy-safe default
Quote 0–50% onboarding · 0 waived months as the starting point. Higher OB should be justified by payback requirements, not churn reduction — it does not reduce churn in the new model.
Default
0–25% onboarding, 0 waived months. Lowest churn, best win rate. Slowest upfront payback. No approval needed.
Acceptable
25–75% onboarding, 0 waived months. Win rate starts declining above 50% but is still viable. Use when faster payback is needed and pipeline can absorb lower win rate.
Acceptable
0–25% onboarding, 1 waived month. Small churn increase (~6 pp). Useful for competitive deals where a free month helps close.
Exception
OB above 75% or 2 waived months — requires explicit justification. Win rate will be at or near the 5% model floor. Pipeline economics must be modeled in Forecast tab.
Avoid
3+ waived months, or any combination where Forecast tab shows net pipeline value negative.

5
Model confidence & caveats
Read this before treating any single prediction as truth.
›

This is a directional decision-support model, not an account-level forecast. Decisions should rest on relative comparisons and guardrails, not point predictions.

Stable, modest fitThe compact churn regression is stable (no NaN standard errors, N = 14,208) but pseudo-R² is only ~0.022. It captures direction well; magnitudes should be treated as approximate.
Waiver-post-sign held at 0The model has a separate term for "onboarding contracted but waived after signing." The dashboard does not separately observe this, so we hold that variable at 0. If you start tracking post-sign waivers, churn predictions for those deals will worsen.
LTV is calibrated, not rawAnalytical LTV from churn survival is multiplied by the calibration factor 0.3854, the median actual/analytical ratio observed historically. This corrects for analytical LTV's tendency to overstate.
Use rankings, not absolute LTVTwo scenarios that differ by $500 of predicted LTV are essentially indistinguishable in this model. Two scenarios that differ by $5,000 are meaningfully different.
